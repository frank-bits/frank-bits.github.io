How to save recipes from screenshots on iPhone

Updated July 2026 · about 4 min read

If you cook, your Camera Roll is probably a graveyard of recipe screenshots — that pasta from Instagram, a friend's text with grandma's cornbread, a half-scrolled blog post you meant to come back to. They're saved, technically. But when it's 6pm and you actually want to make one, they're impossible to find.

The fix isn't to retype everything into Notes. It's to move those screenshots into a proper recipe keeper — an app built to store, organize, and cook from the recipes you've already captured. Here's how to do it in a couple of minutes.

Why recipe screenshots don't work long-term

  • They aren't searchable. iOS can OCR text in photos, but you still can't tag, sort, or plan meals around a screenshot.
  • They get buried. Every new photo pushes last week's recipe further down the roll.
  • They lose context. A screenshot of step 4 is useless without steps 1–3 and the ingredient list.

Step 1 — Round up the recipes you've already saved

Open Photos and search "recipe," "ingredients," or the name of a dish. iOS text recognition will surface most of your recipe screenshots. Don't worry about deleting anything yet — you're just seeing how much is scattered around.

Step 2 — Import screenshots into a recipe box

This is the step that actually solves the problem. Instead of retyping, use a recipe keeper that imports recipes from screenshots, photos, and web pages. In SavorKeep, you point it at a screenshot and it pulls the recipe into a clean, editable card — title, ingredients, and steps — that lives in your searchable recipe collection.

Step 3 — Tag and organize so you can find recipes later

Once a recipe is in, add a tag or two — "weeknight," "vegetarian," "Mom's," whatever matches how you actually think about food. A recipe organizer earns its keep the moment you can pull up "all my quick dinners" in one tap instead of scrolling 400 photos.

Step 4 — Turn saved recipes into a meal plan and grocery list

The real payoff of keeping recipes in one place: you can plan the week from them. Drop a few saved recipes onto a meal planner, and let the app roll their ingredients into a single grocery list grouped for a faster shop. The screenshot you took three months ago finally becomes dinner.
